Tenses and Voice in Scientific Writing (Biology)

Mastering the appropriate use of tenses and voice is crucial for clear, concise, and professional scientific communication in biology manuscripts, reports, and presentations.

1. Understanding Tenses in Scientific Context

Tenses indicate when an action occurred. In scientific writing, specific tenses are preferred for different types of information.

1.1. Present Simple Tense

Used for:

  • General truths, facts, and established scientific principles.
  • Referring to figures, tables, or sections within the manuscript.
  • Stating conclusions or interpretations that are generally true.

Example 1 (General Truth): Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants convert light energy into chemical energy.

Example 2 (Referring to Manuscript): Figure 3 shows the growth curve of E. coli under different conditions.

Example 3 (Conclusion): Our results suggest that gene X plays a critical role in cellular differentiation.

1.2. Past Simple Tense

Used for:

  • Describing specific experimental procedures (Methods section).
  • Presenting your results (what you observed or measured).
  • Referring to specific past research or events.

Example 1 (Methods): Cells were cultured in DMEM medium supplemented with 10% FBS.

Example 2 (Results): The treatment group exhibited a significant increase in biomass compared to the control.

Example 3 (Past Research): Mendel discovered the basic principles of heredity in the 19th century.

1.3. Present Perfect Tense

Used for:

  • Actions that started in the past and continue to the present.
  • Past actions that have a present relevance or consequence.
  • Summarizing previous research or findings (often in Introduction).

Example 1 (Continuing Action): Research on CRISPR-Cas9 technology has advanced rapidly in recent years.

Example 2 (Past Action, Present Relevance): Our experiments have confirmed the hypothesis proposed by Smith et al. (2020).

Example 3 (Summarizing Research): Numerous studies have investigated the effects of climate change on marine ecosystems.

1.4. Future Simple Tense

Used for:

  • Discussing future work, implications, or predictions based on your findings.

Example 1 (Future Work): Future studies will focus on the molecular mechanisms underlying this interaction.

Example 2 (Implication/Prediction): This discovery will open new avenues for therapeutic development.

2. Understanding Voice: Active vs. Passive

Voice indicates whether the subject of the verb performs or receives the action. Both active and passive voices have their place in scientific writing.

2.1. Active Voice

In active voice, the subject performs the action. It is generally preferred for clarity, conciseness, and emphasizing the actor (e.g., the researcher or a specific gene).

  • Structure: Subject + Verb + Object
  • When to use:
    • To emphasize the agent (who or what performed the action).
    • For clarity and directness, especially in the Introduction, Discussion, and Conclusion.
    • When referring to your own actions (e.g., "We analyzed the data...").

Example 1: We incubated the cells for 24 hours. (Clear who performed the action)

Example 2: The enzyme catalyzes the reaction. (Emphasizes the enzyme's role)

Example 3: Smith et al. (2020) reported similar findings.

2.2. Passive Voice

In passive voice, the subject receives the action. It is often used when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or when the focus is on the action or the object being acted upon.

  • Structure: Object + "to be" verb + Past Participle + (by Agent - optional)
  • When to use:
    • To emphasize the action or the recipient of the action, rather than the performer.
    • When the agent is unknown or irrelevant.
    • Commonly used in the Materials and Methods section to describe experimental procedures objectively.

Example 1: The cells were incubated for 24 hours. (Focus on the cells and incubation, not who did it)

Example 2: The reaction is catalyzed by an unknown enzyme. (Agent unknown)

Example 3: Similar findings were reported by Smith et al. (2020). (Slightly less direct than active)

2.3. Active vs. Passive: When to Choose?

While passive voice has been traditionally common in scientific writing, especially in Methods, many journals and style guides now encourage the use of active voice for greater clarity and conciseness, particularly in the Introduction, Results, and Discussion sections.

  • Generally prefer Active: For clarity, conciseness, and when you want to emphasize the agent (e.g., "We observed...", "The protein binds...").
  • Use Passive when appropriate: When the agent is truly unknown or unimportant, or when the focus must be on the action or object (e.g., "Samples were collected...", "Data were analyzed...").

3. Tenses and Voice in Manuscript Sections

The choice of tense and voice often varies depending on the section of your scientific manuscript or report.

3.1. Abstract

  • Purpose: Summarize the entire study.
  • Tenses/Voice:
    • Background/Objective: Present simple ("This study investigates...").
    • Methods/Results: Past simple (often passive) ("Cells were treated...", "Results showed...").
    • Conclusions: Present simple ("Our findings suggest...", "This research highlights...").

Example: The effects of nutrient limitation on algal growth were investigated. We found that biomass production decreased significantly under phosphate depletion. These results indicate the critical role of phosphate in algal blooms.

3.2. Introduction

  • Purpose: Provide background, state the problem, review literature, and state the hypothesis/objective.
  • Tenses/Voice:
    • General Background/Facts: Present simple ("DNA replication occurs...", "Climate change is a global issue...").
    • Previous Research: Present perfect (for ongoing relevance, "Studies have shown...") or Past simple (for specific past studies, "Smith et al. (2020) reported...").
    • Gap/Problem/Hypothesis/Objective: Present simple ("However, the mechanism remains unclear.", "This study aims to elucidate...", "We hypothesize that...").

Example: Apoptosis is a fundamental biological process. Recent research has highlighted the involvement of microRNAs in its regulation. However, the specific role of miR-123 in liver cell apoptosis is not fully understood. Therefore, this study investigates the impact of miR-123 on apoptosis in hepatocellular carcinoma cells.

3.3. Materials and Methods

  • Purpose: Describe how the study was conducted, allowing for replication.
  • Tenses/Voice:
    • Primarily Past Simple Passive: To emphasize the procedures and materials, not the researchers. ("Cells were harvested...", "DNA was extracted...", "Statistical analysis was performed...").
    • Past Simple Active (less common but acceptable): If you want to emphasize your specific actions, especially in "We" statements. ("We prepared the samples...", "We then analyzed the data...").

Example: Plant samples were collected from three different sites. DNA was extracted using a commercial kit. PCR amplification was performed with specific primers. The resulting amplicons were sequenced, and the data were analyzed using bioinformatics software.

3.4. Results

  • Purpose: Present the findings objectively.
  • Tenses/Voice:
    • Primarily Past Simple: For describing what you observed or measured. ("The growth rate increased...", "We observed a significant reduction...").
    • Present Simple: When referring to figures/tables, or stating universally true observations from your data. ("Figure 1 shows...", "The data indicate...").

Example: The control group showed a consistent growth pattern (Figure 2A). In contrast, the treated group exhibited a 40% reduction in cell viability. These findings suggest a dose-dependent effect of the compound.

3.5. Discussion

  • Purpose: Interpret results, relate them to previous research, discuss limitations, and suggest future directions.
  • Tenses/Voice:
    • Referring to your results: Past simple ("Our results demonstrated...", "We found that...").
    • Interpreting results, general truths, implications: Present simple ("This suggests that...", "The findings are consistent with...", "Gene X plays a crucial role...").
    • Referring to previous research: Past simple or Present perfect ("Smith et al. (2020) reported...", "Other studies have shown...").
    • Future work/recommendations: Future simple ("Future research will investigate...", "Further studies are needed...").

Example: Our findings are consistent with previous studies that have identified this pathway. The observed decrease in viability could be attributed to mitochondrial dysfunction. Further research will explore the specific molecular targets involved.

3.6. Conclusion

  • Purpose: Briefly summarize the main findings and their significance.
  • Tenses/Voice:
    • Primarily Present Simple: For stating the main conclusions and their broader implications. ("This study demonstrates...", "Our research provides new insights...").

Example: In conclusion, this study definitively shows that compound Z inhibits bacterial growth, offering a potential new strategy for antimicrobial development.

5. Key Takeaways

  • Present Simple: For general truths, referring to figures, and conclusions.
  • Past Simple: For methods and results (what you did and what happened).
  • Present Perfect: For actions started in the past with present relevance, or summarizing previous research.
  • Active Voice: Preferred for clarity, conciseness, and emphasizing the actor (especially in Introduction, Results, Discussion).
  • Passive Voice: Useful when the actor is unknown/unimportant, or the focus is on the action/object (common in Methods).
  • Context is Key: The best choice depends on the specific section of your manuscript and what you want to emphasize.
